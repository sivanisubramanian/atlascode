Quick Reference

Everything you need fast on the sideline. No fluff.

Concussion Red Flags
Remove from play immediately if any present
Headache or pressure Confusion / fog Memory gaps Sensitivity to light or noise Balance problems Nausea or vomiting Unequal pupils Loss of consciousness Slurred speech
When in doubt, sit them out. No exceptions.
Heat Illness Spectrum
Know the difference — heat stroke is life-threatening
Heat Cramps
Painful muscle spasms, usually legs or abdomen. Still sweating. Alert and oriented.
Rest, fluids, gentle stretching
Heat Exhaustion
Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, fast weak pulse, nausea.
Cool environment, fluids, monitor closely
Heat Stroke — above 104°F
Confusion, altered consciousness, may stop sweating. Hot red skin. This is an emergency.
Call 911. Cold water immersion immediately. Cool first, transport second.
When to Call 911
Don't hesitate — call and let EMS decide
Suspected heat stroke Loss of consciousness Suspected spinal injury Cardiac arrest / no pulse Seizure Difficulty breathing Uncontrolled bleeding Suspected fracture with deformity Unequal pupils after head impact
When calling: state your location first, then what happened.
Wound Care Steps
For cuts and abrasions on the sideline
1Gloves on before touching the wound
2Irrigate with saline or clean water — remove all debris
3Assess depth — if deep or won't stop bleeding, refer for stitches
4Apply appropriate dressing — larger pad for joints
5Secure with athletic tape or Coban — bridge across joint if needed
6Check circulation — compression should never feel tight or numb
Taping Quick Notes
Key things to remember before you tape
1Always apply pre-wrap first — protects the skin
2Ankle tape: start a few inches above the ankle bone, no wrinkles
3Wrist tape: anchor on mid-forearm, figure-8 around the wrist
4Buddy tape fingers: pad between fingers first, tape between joints — not over them
5After taping: check circulation — athlete should have normal feeling and color
